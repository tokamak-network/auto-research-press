### Response to Reviewer 1 (Respiratory Medicine)

**Overall Assessment**: We are encouraged by the reviewer's positive evaluation (Average: 8.2/10) and recognition that our manuscript "effectively highlights the disproportionate burden of indoor cooking smoke on women, particularly regarding COPD risk." We fully agree that deeper integration of respiratory pathophysiology and lung function metrics would substantially strengthen the clinical relevance of our work.

**Major Points**:

1. **Limited discussion of objective lung function metrics and spirometry-based COPD definitions**
   - **Our response**: This is an excellent point. We focused too heavily on epidemiological associations without adequately describing the clinical lung function phenotypes that characterize biomass smoke-induced COPD. The reviewer is correct that spirometric criteria are central to COPD diagnosis and should be explicitly discussed.
   - **Action taken**: We will add a new subsection (150-200 words) titled "Lung Function Impairment and Spirometric Patterns" that includes:
     * Post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC < 0.7 diagnostic threshold for airflow obstruction
     * Specific data on FEV1 decline rates in women with chronic biomass exposure (we will cite Regalado et al. 2006, *Eur Respir J*, showing 33 mL/year greater decline; and Ramírez-Venegas et al. 2006, *Chest*, documenting obstructive patterns in biomass-exposed Mexican women)
     * Discussion of mixed obstructive-restrictive patterns and small airway disease characteristic of biomass smoke exposure (distinct from tobacco COPD)
     * Reduced DLCO (diffusion capacity) as a marker of parenchymal damage

2. **Lack of specific citations for numerical estimates (e.g., '50-90% PM2.5 reduction' from improved cookstoves)**
   - **Our response**: The reviewer is absolutely right that quantitative claims require direct citation. This ties to Reviewer 2's broader concern about citation verification. The 50-90% range reflects variability across stove types and study conditions but was presented without adequate source documentation.
   - **Action taken**: We will:
     * Cite specific intervention studies with measured PM2.5 reductions: Mortimer et al. 2017 (*Lancet*, RESPIRE Malawi trial: ~40% personal PM2.5 reduction with improved biomass stoves); Clasen et al. 2020 (*NEJM*, HAPIN trial: LPG intervention achieved 63% reduction in personal PM2.5)
     * Clarify that laboratory/controlled conditions often show higher reductions (70-90%) than field settings (30-60%) due to stove stacking, maintenance, and behavioral factors
     * Remove unsupported numerical claims or replace with cited data

3. **Need for expanded mechanistic pathways linking PM2.5 to lung damage**
   - **Our response**: We agree this would deepen the physiological context and help clinicians understand why biomass smoke causes COPD in never-smokers. The current manuscript mentions inflammation but lacks mechanistic detail.
   - **Action taken**: We will expand the pathophysiology discussion (200-250 words) to include:
     * Alveolar deposition of ultrafine particles (PM0.1) and carbon black
     * Macrophage activation and neutrophilic airway inflammation (distinct from eosinophilic asthma)
     * Oxidative stress pathways: reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation, glutathione depletion, lipid peroxidation
     * Protease/antiprotease imbalance: increased matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), reduced alpha-1 antitrypsin activity leading to emphysematous changes
     * Epithelial barrier dysfunction and accelerated lung aging (telomere shortening, cellular senescence)
     * We will cite key mechanistic studies: Salvi & Barnes 2009 (*Eur Respir J*), Hosgood et al. 2012 (*Thorax*), and Gordon et al. 2014 (*Respir Res*)

4. **Clarification needed on '20 years of exposure doubles COPD risk' claim**
   - **Our response**: The reviewer correctly identifies imprecision here. We implied this duration-risk gradient from Hu et al. 2010 but that meta-analysis reports pooled ORs without explicit duration stratification at 20 years.
   - **Action taken**: We will revise to accurately reflect the cited evidence:
     * Hu et al. 2010 (*Lancet*): pooled OR 2.44 (95% CI 1.9-3.33) for COPD from biomass smoke, without specific duration threshold
     * For duration-response, we will cite Po et al. 2011 (*Thorax*): OR 1.04 per 10 year-hours/day exposure increase
     * Liu et al. 2007 (*Chest*): women with >30 years biomass exposure had OR 3.4 vs. <10 years
     * We will reframe as: "Risk increases with cumulative exposure duration, with studies showing 2-3 fold elevated COPD risk after several decades of daily cooking"

**Minor Points**:
- **Integration of recent trial data (RESPIRE, PURE-AIR)**: Excellent suggestion. We will add discussion of RESPIRE (Guatemala/Malawi improved stoves) and HAPIN (LPG intervention across four countries) trials in the intervention section, noting that despite PM2.5 reductions, these trials showed limited short-term health benefits, highlighting the challenge of achieving WHO guideline levels and the importance of clean fuel transitions.
- **Clinical terminology precision**: We will consistently use "post-bronchodilator spirometry," "airflow limitation," and "obstructive/restrictive patterns" rather than vague "lung function decline."

2. **Lack of quantitative rigor and statistical context for exposure-disease associations**
   - **Our response**: The reviewer correctly notes that we mention effect sizes but lack proper statistical context, confidence intervals, and discussion of heterogeneity. This reflects insufficient critical evaluation of the evidence base.
   - **Action taken**: We will revise all health outcome sections to include:
     * **Precise effect estimates with 95% CIs**: e.g., "COPD: pooled OR 2.44 (95% CI 1.90-3.33) from meta-analysis of 15 studies (Hu et al. 2010); significant heterogeneity (I²=84%) due to variation in exposure metrics and COPD definitions"
     * **Dose-response gradients where available**: e.g., "Cardiovascular mortality: HR 1.12 (95% CI 1.03-1.21) per 10 µg/m³ increase in PM2.5 from cooking emissions (cited from verified cohort study)"
     * **Stratified analyses**: Present separate estimates for biomass vs. coal vs. gas exposures when data permit
     * **Discussion of heterogeneity sources**: variation in exposure assessment (self-reported fuel vs. PM measurements), outcome definitions (spirometry vs. physician diagnosis), and population characteristics

3. **No critical evaluation of study quality, biases, or evidence limitations**
   - **Our response**: This is a major gap. We presented findings uncritically without discussing study design limitations, bias sources, or strength of causal inference. The reviewer's specific examples (recall bias, confounding by SES, exposure misclassification) are all highly relevant to this literature.
   - **Action taken**: We will add a new section titled **"Methodological Considerations and Evidence Quality"** (300-400 words) addressing:
     * **Study design hierarchy**: Distinguish cross-sectional (prevalence associations), case-control (retrospective exposure, recall bias), and prospective cohort (temporal sequence, repeated exposure assessment) studies. Note that most evidence is observational; randomized trials of clean fuel interventions provide stronger causal inference but often short follow-up.
     * **Confounding by SES**: Fuel choice is strongly correlated with poverty, education, nutrition, healthcare access, and sanitation. Discuss how well studies control for these (e.g., education-stratified analyses, within-household fuel switching studies, propensity score matching). Acknowledge residual confounding as a major limitation.
     * **Exposure misclassification**: Self-reported fuel type is crude proxy for actual PM2.5 exposure due to: stove efficiency variation, cooking duration, ventilation differences, outdoor air pollution infiltration, and stove stacking (concurrent use of multiple fuel types). Studies with personal PM monitoring provide better exposure assessment but are fewer. Misclassification typically biases toward null, suggesting true effects may be underestimated.
     * **Recall bias**: Particularly problematic for reproductive outcomes (e.g., asking about fuel use during pregnancy years after delivery) and retrospective case-control studies of chronic diseases.
     * **Outcome ascertainment**: Spirometry-confirmed COPD vs. self-reported diagnosis vs. symptom-based definitions vary widely in specificity. Cardiovascular events may be under-diagnosed in low-resource settings.

4. **Lack of discussion on exposure assessment heterogeneity and dose-response relationships**
   - **Our response**: The reviewer is right that different studies use incomparable exposure metrics (fuel type vs. cooking hours vs. PM measurements), making synthesis difficult. We also did not adequately present actual exposure levels associated with health outcomes.
   - **Action taken**: We will:
     * Add a table or detailed text summarizing **exposure levels by source**: e.g., "Traditional biomass cookstoves: 24-hr kitchen PM2.5 200-2000 µg/m³, personal exposure 100-500 µg/m³; improved biomass stoves: 50-300 µg/m³; LPG: 20-80 µg/m³; gas stoves (high-income): 30-100 µg/m³ during cooking events" (all values with citations)
     * Discuss **threshold effects**: "No safe threshold identified for PM2.5; health effects observed at all exposure levels. WHO guideline (24-hr PM2.5 15 µg/m³) rarely achieved even with clean fuels due to outdoor pollution and other indoor sources"
     * Present **exposure-response functions** where available: e.g., log-linear relationships for mortality, potential non-linearity at very high exposures
     * Acknowledge that **different metrics** (fuel type, cooking hours, PM2.5, CO, BC, PAHs) capture different aspects of exposure and are not directly comparable

5. **Lack of discussion on effect modification and intervention effectiveness**
   - **Our response**: The reviewer notes that ventilation, cooking duration, and fuel moisture content modify exposure-outcome relationships, and that improved cookstove trials have shown disappointing health benefits. These are critical nuances missing from our manuscript.
   - **Action taken**: We will:
     * Add discussion of **effect modifiers**: ventilation (natural vs. mechanical), housing characteristics (enclosed vs. open kitchens), cooking location (indoor vs. outdoor), cooking duration (hours/day), household size (multiple cooks), and fuel quality (dry vs. wet biomass)
     * Critically evaluate **intervention evidence**: "Improved biomass cookstove trials (RESPIRE, CRECER) achieved 30-60% PM2.5 reductions but showed limited health benefits in short-term follow-up, possibly due to: (a) insufficient exposure reduction to reach WHO guidelines, (b) continued stove stacking, (c) short follow-up for chronic outcomes, (d) exposure from non-cooking sources. Clean fuel transitions (LPG, electricity) achieve greater exposure reductions but face affordability and access barriers."
     * Cite HAPIN trial (Clasen et al. 2022, *NEJM*): LPG intervention reduced personal PM2.5 by 63% but did not significantly reduce low birth weight or child pneumonia in 1-year follow-up, highlighting complexity of intervention impacts

**Minor Points**:
- **Temporal relationships for reproductive outcomes**: Agreed. We will clarify that exposure assessment must occur during pregnancy (ideally by trimester) for reproductive outcomes, and discuss limitations of studies using lifetime fuel use as proxy for pregnancy exposure.
- **Separating cooking smoke from other pollution sources**: We will acknowledge the challenge of attributing effects specifically to cooking emissions when households are exposed to multiple sources (heating, lighting, outdoor air pollution, environmental tobacco smoke, occupational exposures).
