   - **Action taken**: The expanded conclusion (1,100 words) now provides the systematic synthesis you required. It explicitly addresses your question "what makes chastened utilitarianism recognizably utilitarian rather than a different theory" by specifying three non-negotiable core commitments: (1) welfarist axiology (all value ultimately derives from effects on sentient welfare), (2) aggregative structure (social welfare is some function of individual welfare, even if prioritarian-weighted), and (3) consequentialist criterion (rightness fundamentally concerns outcomes rather than intrinsic properties of acts/rules). The conclusion argues that qualifications (rights, prerogatives, constraints) represent the boundary conditions for implementing these commitments in contexts of uncertainty, limited information, and imperfect motivation—they emerge from second-order consequentialist reasoning rather than compromising foundational principles. It also explicitly addresses which critiques have been fully addressed (simple hedonism, crude aggregation, ignoring distribution), which have been substantially mitigated (demandingness, separateness of persons, rights), and which remain genuinely problematic (value incommensurability, moral uncertainty across frameworks, deep disagreement about welfare conceptions).

2. **Rights-as-institutional-rules requires deeper development**
   - **Our response**: Your concern that "the distinction between second-order consequentialist justification and instrumental justification is not fully clear" identifies a genuine weakness in our Round 2 draft. You ask the crucial question: "If rights are justified because adopting them maximizes welfare, in what sense are they not instrumental?"
   - **Action taken**: We have substantially developed this argument (additional 400 words in the rights section, integrated discussion in conclusion). We now explicitly distinguish three positions: (a) rights as mere instruments (strategic devices we adopt when convenient but abandon when direct calculation promises better outcomes), (b) rights as institutional rules justified through second-order consequentialism (constraints we adopt because systematic adherence produces better outcomes than case-by-case calculation, even when violating them in particular cases would produce better immediate outcomes), and (c) rights as non-consequentially grounded constraints (limits on what we may do to persons independent of consequences). We defend position (b) as recognizably consequentialist while capturing important features of moral reality: the epistemic and motivational limitations that make direct optimization unreliable, the coordination benefits of stable expectations, and the welfare costs of institutional instability. We draw on Hooker's (2000) rule-consequentialism and Pettit's (1997) distinction between consequentialism as outcome-promoting and consequentialism as outcome-honoring to clarify how second-order justification differs from mere instrumentalism. The key insight is that rights function as *constitutive features* of optimal moral frameworks rather than *optional tools* we can discard—they are instrumental to welfare maximization in the sense that their systematic implementation promotes better outcomes, but they are not mere instruments in the sense that violating them whenever direct calculation suggests better immediate results would undermine the framework's optimality.
