

# AI and Cryptocurrency Market Microstructure: A Critical Methodological Review of Prediction, MEV Extraction, and Algorithmic Trading

## Abstract

This paper provides a critical methodological review of artificial intelligence applications in cryptocurrency market microstructure, with a focused contribution: a systematic assessment of backtesting practices, evaluation protocols, and technical architectures in the AI-crypto trading literature, identifying prevalent methodological weaknesses that undermine reported performance claims. Rather than surveying the full breadth of AI-crypto intersections, this review narrows its scope to three interconnected domains where technical depth is most needed: price prediction methodologies and their evaluation rigor, AI-driven market microstructure effects including Maximal Extractable Value (MEV) extraction and automated market maker (AMM) dynamics, and fraud detection on decentralized versus centralized exchanges. Drawing on peer-reviewed empirical studies, the analysis reveals that reported prediction accuracies are frequently inflated by inadequate validation protocols, that AI-powered MEV bots have fundamentally restructured on-chain market microstructure, and that regulatory frameworks including the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) remain insufficiently adapted to algorithmic trading in decentralized environments [1, 6, 7]. The paper concludes with empirically grounded assessments of near-term trajectories for AI-crypto integration.

## 1. Introduction

Cryptocurrency markets exhibit distinctive statistical properties—extreme volatility, heavy-tailed return distributions, and time-varying cross-asset correlations—that make them both attractive targets and formidable challenges for artificial intelligence applications [6, 7]. Burnie [6] demonstrated through correlation network analysis that cryptocurrency interconnectedness intensifies during market stress, while Krafft, Della Penna, and Pentland [7] showed experimentally that participant behavior is heavily shaped by social information and herding effects, introducing reflexive dynamics where prediction models can alter the phenomena they seek to predict.

The existing literature on AI in cryptocurrency markets suffers from two systemic problems that this review aims to address. First, prediction studies frequently report impressive backtest performance without adequate methodological controls for overfitting, data leakage, and transaction costs, rendering their claims unreliable as guides for actual trading performance. Second, discussions of AI's market microstructure impact typically remain at a superficial level, failing to engage with the technical specifics of how AI agents operate on-chain—particularly regarding MEV extraction, AMM liquidity provision, and mempool dynamics. This review contributes a structured critical assessment of both problems, offering a methodological framework for evaluating AI-crypto research claims and a technically grounded analysis of AI's role in on-chain market microstructure.

## 2. AI-Driven Price Prediction: Architectures, Metrics, and Methodological Pitfalls

### 2.1 Evolution of Prediction Architectures

The application of machine learning to cryptocurrency price prediction has progressed through several methodological generations. Early approaches adapted traditional time-series methods from equity markets, including ARIMA models and basic feedforward neural networks. Penteado [10] provided statistical evidence supporting the informational content of technical indicators, motivating the incorporation of technical features into subsequent ML-based forecasting models. A comprehensive study published in Frontiers in Artificial Intelligence evaluated multiple deep learning approaches for Bitcoin price prediction, reporting that hybrid CNN-LSTM architectures outperformed single-architecture baselines [12, 14]. These hybrids leverage CNNs for extracting local patterns from price and volume data while utilizing LSTMs to capture long-range temporal dependencies.

However, the reported performance of these models requires careful scrutiny. The Frontiers study [14] employed standard train-test splits on historical Bitcoin data, but critical methodological details—including whether walk-forward validation was used, whether transaction costs and slippage were incorporated, and whether the test period included regime changes—substantially affect the reliability of reported metrics. This concern is not unique to that study; it reflects a pervasive pattern in the crypto-AI prediction literature where models are evaluated under conditions that do not approximate live trading environments.

Transformer-based architectures and large language models have opened new frontiers by enabling integration of unstructured data sources [3]. Roumeliotis and Tselikas [3] reviewed the capabilities of ChatGPT and related models for natural language understanding applicable to financial sentiment analysis. In the cryptocurrency context, LLMs can process social media discourse, news articles, and regulatory announcements to generate sentiment signals complementing quantitative features. Yet the critical question—whether LLM-derived sentiment signals provide statistically significant alpha after transaction costs—remains largely unanswered in the peer-reviewed literature. LLMs are prone to hallucination and overconfident predictions, and their application to financial forecasting introduces risks of spurious signal generation that backtests may not capture.

### 2.2 Critical Assessment of Backtesting Practices

The most significant methodological concern in AI-crypto prediction research is the prevalence of flawed backtesting protocols. Based on a critical reading of the literature, including the studies reviewed in [12, 14], several recurring weaknesses can be identified.

Regarding data leakage and look-ahead bias, many studies normalize or scale features using statistics computed over the entire dataset, including future observations, rather than using only information available at prediction time. This introduces subtle look-ahead bias that inflates reported accuracy. Walk-forward validation—where models are retrained on expanding windows and tested only on subsequent out-of-sample periods—is the appropriate protocol for non-stationary financial time series, yet a substantial portion of crypto-AI papers rely on simple random or chronological train-test splits without rolling retraining.

Concerning unrealistic performance assumptions, studies that report directional accuracy or mean squared error without accounting for bid-ask spreads, exchange fees, slippage, and market impact provide misleading assessments of tradable alpha. In cryptocurrency markets, where spreads on less liquid pairs can exceed 50 basis points and market impact for larger orders is substantial, these omissions can transform apparently profitable strategies into loss-making ones.

The problem of non-stationarity and regime change is also critical. Cryptocurrency markets undergo frequent regime shifts driven by regulatory interventions, technological upgrades such as Ethereum's transition to proof-of-stake, and macroeconomic shifts. Models trained during one regime may fail catastrophically in another. Krafft et al. [7] demonstrated that cryptocurrency market dynamics are heavily influenced by social information cascades, creating reflexive feedback loops where the deployment of popular prediction models can erode the very patterns they exploit through crowding effects.

These methodological concerns do not invalidate AI-based prediction research entirely, but they demand that reported results be interpreted with substantial caution. Future work should adopt standardized evaluation protocols that include walk-forward validation with realistic transaction cost models, out-of-sample testing across distinct market regimes, and explicit reporting of risk-adjusted metrics such as Sharpe ratios net of costs.

## 3. AI Agents and On-Chain Market Microstructure

### 3.1 MEV Extraction and AI-Powered Bots

The most consequential intersection of AI and cryptocurrency market microstructure is Maximal Extractable Value (MEV)—the profit that can be extracted by reordering, inserting, or censoring transactions within a block. Since the foundational analysis by Daian et al. (2020), which documented how automated bots exploit mempool visibility to frontrun, backrun, and sandwich ordinary users' transactions on decentralized exchanges, the MEV ecosystem has become a primary domain for AI-driven market manipulation and arbitrage. AI-powered MEV bots employ reinforcement learning to optimize transaction ordering strategies in real time, monitoring the mempool for pending transactions and calculating profitable insertion points for sandwich attacks—where a bot places a buy order before a target transaction and a sell order after it, profiting from the price impact at the target's expense.

The scale of MEV extraction is substantial. Flashbots data indicate billions of dollars in cumulative extracted value on Ethereum, representing a direct transfer from ordinary users to sophisticated algorithmic actors. This dynamic fundamentally challenges the democratizing ethos of decentralized finance, as AI-equipped agents systematically extract value from less sophisticated participants. The development of Flashbots Protect and similar private transaction relay services represents a partial mitigation, but the arms race between MEV extraction and MEV protection continues to drive innovation in on-chain AI systems.

### 3.2 AMM Dynamics and AI-Optimized Liquidity Provision

Automated market makers on decentralized exchanges employ deterministic pricing functions—most commonly the constant product formula (x × y = k) pioneered by Uniswap—that replace traditional order book dynamics. The introduction of concentrated liquidity in Uniswap v3 transformed liquidity provision into an active management problem, where providers must select price ranges and rebalance positions in response to market movements. This optimization problem is well-suited to AI approaches: reinforcement learning agents can learn to dynamically adjust liquidity positions to maximize fee income while minimizing impermanent loss, the opportunity cost incurred when asset prices diverge from the price at which liquidity was provided.

AI-driven liquidity provision strategies interact with MEV dynamics in complex ways. Sophisticated AI agents can simultaneously provide liquidity, execute arbitrage between DEXs and centralized exchanges (CEXs), and extract MEV, creating vertically integrated algorithmic operations that dominate on-chain market making. Empirical evidence suggests that AI-driven arbitrage bots improve cross-venue price convergence between CEXs and DEXs, but this efficiency gain comes at the cost of value extraction from passive liquidity providers and ordinary users.

### 3.3 DEX versus CEX Microstructure Implications

The distinction between decentralized and centralized exchange microstructure has critical implications for AI deployment. On CEXs, AI trading systems operate within conventional order book environments with sub-millisecond latency, where the primary competitive advantage is speed and signal processing. On DEXs, the competitive landscape is fundamentally different: transaction ordering is determined by block producers rather than arrival time, mempool visibility creates information asymmetries exploitable by AI bots, and smart contract interactions introduce composability risks where AI agents can chain multiple protocol interactions within a single transaction. AI-driven liquidation bots operating on DeFi lending protocols such as Aave and Compound monitor collateralization ratios in real time, competing to liquidate undercollateralized positions for profit. The interaction of multiple AI liquidation agents during periods of rapid price decline can create cascading liquidation events that amplify volatility—a systemic risk mechanism that has manifested in several notable DeFi market dislocations.

## 4. Security, Fraud Detection, and Market Integrity

AI has become essential for addressing security challenges in cryptocurrency ecosystems [9]. Phillips and Wilder [9] demonstrated how clustering analysis of replicated scam websites can identify fraudulent operations at scale. Beyond website analysis, graph neural networks applied to blockchain transaction data enable tracing of funds through complex obfuscation chains, including mixing services and layered transactions. The application of AI to wash trading detection is particularly important given the historical prevalence of fraudulent volume reporting on cryptocurrency exchanges. Machine learning approaches analyzing transaction graph topology, timing patterns, and wallet clustering can identify wash trading with increasing accuracy on both CEXs and DEXs, though false positive rates and adversarial robustness remain significant challenges.

The audit and verification dimensions of AI in cryptocurrency markets parallel developments documented by Fedyk et al. [5] in traditional accounting contexts. AI-enhanced tools can verify exchange reserve claims and analyze the financial health of DeFi protocols, capabilities that are critical given the opacity of many protocols' financial positions. However, adversarial machine learning techniques can craft transactions designed to evade detection systems, while LLM-generated content enables increasingly sophisticated social engineering attacks targeting cryptocurrency holders [3, 9].

## 5. Regulatory Frameworks and Ethical Dimensions

The regulatory landscape for AI in cryptocurrency markets is evolving rapidly but remains fragmented. The EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA), which entered full application in 2024, represents the most comprehensive framework globally, establishing licensing requirements for crypto-asset service providers and market abuse prohibitions that implicitly cover algorithmic trading practices. However, MiCA does not specifically address AI-driven MEV extraction or autonomous on-chain agents, leaving significant regulatory gaps. In the United States, SEC enforcement actions against major exchanges including Binance and Coinbase, alongside ongoing debates about whether DeFi protocols constitute exchanges or broker-dealers, create regulatory uncertainty that directly affects how AI trading systems can legally operate. The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) Travel Rule requirements for virtual asset service providers add a compliance layer that AI systems must navigate, particularly for cross-border transactions.

Thiebes, Lins, and Sunyaev [1] developed a framework for trustworthy AI emphasizing fairness, transparency, accountability, and privacy. Applying this framework to AI-driven cryptocurrency trading reveals substantial gaps: most algorithmic trading systems operate as opaque black boxes, the concentration of AI capabilities among well-resourced firms raises fairness concerns, and accountability for algorithmic failures remains unclear [1]. The environmental dimension is also significant: the computational demands of both AI model training and blockchain operations create compounded environmental costs, though the transition toward proof-of-stake consensus mechanisms partially mitigates the blockchain component [4].

## 6. Empirically Grounded Future Assessment

Rather than speculative predictions, several near-term trajectories can be assessed based on observable trends and existing technical capabilities. The integration of autonomous AI agents into DeFi protocols—already operational through projects such as Autonolas and Fetch.ai—is shifting AI from a tool used by human traders to an independent market participant, raising novel governance and liability questions [1]. The MEV landscape will likely be reshaped by protocol-level mitigations including encrypted mempools and fair ordering mechanisms, forcing AI agents to adapt their strategies. Advances in explainable AI will be critical for regulatory compliance under frameworks like MiCA, as algorithmic transparency requirements become enforceable [1]. Finally, the development of AI systems specifically designed for real-time market surveillance will become a regulatory priority, building on the fraud detection capabilities documented in the current literature [5, 9].

## 7. Conclusion

This review has identified two critical gaps in the AI-cryptocurrency literature: the prevalence of methodologically flawed backtesting practices that inflate reported prediction performance, and the insufficient technical engagement with on-chain market microstructure dynamics including MEV extraction and AMM optimization. Addressing these gaps requires standardized evaluation protocols for prediction research, deeper integration of market microstructure theory with AI systems analysis, and regulatory frameworks specifically adapted to algorithmic agents operating in decentralized environments. The reflexive dynamics documented by Krafft et al. [7]—where AI deployment alters the market phenomena it seeks to predict or exploit—will intensify as AI agents become more prevalent, demanding interdisciplinary collaboration among computer scientists, financial economists, and regulators [1, 4, 7].
